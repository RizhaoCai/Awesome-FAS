💡 Five trends shaping the field

#1 · The defining problem

Generalization eats everything

By far the most common goal: 108 of ~309 paper notes mention domain generalization or cross-dataset robustness. The benchmark question shifted from "can you detect this attack?" to "can you detect the next one?"

#2 · The architecture turn

From CNNs to vision-language & MLLMs

36 papers now lean on CLIP, prompts, or multimodal LLMs — from FLIP/CFPL in anti-spoofing to Forensics-Adapter/M2F2-Det in deepfakes. Foundation models are becoming the shared backbone, and they bring interpretability (FaceShield, I-FAS) as a bonus.

#3 · Generative AI, both sides

Diffusion is tool and threat

Diffusion creates harder fakes (DiFF, diffusion-face benchmarks) and augments detectors (DiffFAS, DiffusionFake). The arms race is now generator-vs-detector, with detectors training on synthetic adversaries.

#4 · Convergence

Physical and digital attacks are merging

Historically separate communities are unifying: a single model that catches a printed photo and a deepfake. UniAttackData, MoAE-CR, and the FSFM foundation model signal a unified attack-detection agenda.

#5 · Beyond the still image

Video, audio & lifelong learning rise

16 audio-visual and 11 continual/incremental papers reflect two realities: deepfakes are video-and-voice, and forgery types keep evolving — so detectors must learn forever without forgetting.

How this list is built

Curated to CCF-A venues (CVPR, ICCV, ECCV, T-PAMI, T-IFS, AAAI, IJCAI, NeurIPS, ICLR, ACM MM), 2022–2026. Every entry is grounded in a verifiable source (proceedings / IEEE-DOI / arXiv) — no auto-generated citations.

Maintained by Rizhao Cai · Contributions welcome via PR/issue on GitHub.

Counts are derived from the curated Markdown lists and reflect a CCF-A-focused selection, not the entire literature. "Type 1 / Type 2" labels follow industry (FIDO) usage; presentation-attack detection itself is standardized in ISO/IEC 30107.
